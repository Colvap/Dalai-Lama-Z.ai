TENZIN GYATSO

His Holiness the Fourteenth Dalai Lama, Tenzin Gyatso, born Lhamo Thondup in Taktser, Amdo. Spiritual leader of the Tibetan people. Nobel laureate. Refugee since 1959. Voice of compassion in an age of noise. A simple Buddhist monk.

Manifesto · Oslo, 11 December 1989

Today, more than ever before, life must be characterized by a sense of universal responsibility. Not only nation to nation and human to human, but also human to other forms of life. In this present circumstance, no one can afford to assume that somebody else will solve our problems. Every individual has a responsibility to help guide our global family in the right direction. Good wishes alone are not enough; we must assume responsibility.

— Tenzin Gyatso, Nobel Peace Prize Lecture, University of Oslo, Norway
Biography · 1935 — present

A life across
two worlds.

From a farming village in Amdo to the Potala Palace. From the Potala across the Himalayas on foot. From exile in Dharamshala to lecture halls on every continent. Eighty-nine years, and counting.

1935 · TAKTSER

A boy born to a farming family in Amdo.

Lhamo Thondup was born on 6 July 1935 to a poor farming family in the small village of Taktser, in the Amdo region of northeastern Tibet. He was the ninth of sixteen children; seven of them would die in childhood. The family kept horses, yaks, and sheep. Two years later, a search party of senior monks, guided by visions and the signs following the death of the 13th Dalai Lama, arrived at the farmhouse disguised as servants. The boy recognized a rosary that had belonged to his predecessor and demanded it be given to him. He was identified as the reincarnation.

1940 · LHASA

Enthroned at the Potala at age five.

On 22 February 1940, Lhamo Thondup was formally enthroned as the 14th Dalai Lama in a ceremony at the Potala Palace in Lhasa. He was given the monastic name Jetsun Jamphel Ngawang Lobsang Yeshe Tenzin Gyatso — Holy Lord, Gentle Glory, Compassionate, Defender of the Faith, Ocean of Wisdom. His education in Buddhist philosophy, logic, Tibetan art, Sanskrit, medicine, and astrology would continue for the next fifteen years under the guidance of senior monks and tutors. He sat for his final examinations at the Jokhang Temple in Lhasa at the age of 23, in 1959, the year of his exile.

1959 · FLIGHT

Crossing the Himalayas into India.

On 10 March 1959, a popular uprising against the Chinese occupation erupted in Lhasa. Tens of thousands surrounded the Norbulingka Palace, fearing for the Dalai Lama's life. On the night of 17 March, the 23-year-old disguised himself as a soldier, left the palace under cover of darkness, and began a fifteen-day journey on foot and on horseback across the Himalayas. He crossed into India on 31 March at the Khenzimane Pass. Approximately 80,000 Tibetans would follow him into exile in the years that followed. He has not returned to Tibet since.

1960 · DHARAMSHALA

Building a nation outside its borders.

In April 1960, the Dalai Lama moved to McLeod Ganj, a small hill station in the Indian state of Himachal Pradesh, where he established the Central Tibetan Administration — the government-in-exile that continues to function from there today. Over the following decades he would establish schools for Tibetan refugee children, monasteries to preserve the philosophical traditions, libraries to archive the literature, agricultural settlements across India and Nepal, and a democratic constitution. He deliberately limited his own authority in favor of elected institutions — a remarkable act for a theocratic ruler.

1989 · OSLO

Awarded the Nobel Peace Prize.

On 5 October 1989, the Norwegian Nobel Committee awarded the Dalai Lama the Nobel Peace Prize. The citation praised "his consistent resistance to the use of violence in his people's struggle to regain their liberty" and noted that "in doing so, he has come to stand forth as a spokesman for peace and understanding in a world which, more than ever, needs such spokesmen." In his acceptance lecture he spoke not of Tibet alone but of universal responsibility, of the interdependence of all life, of the duty of every individual to act.

2011 · DEVOLUTION

Voluntarily surrendering political power.

On 14 March 2011, the Dalai Lama formally devolved his political authority to an elected leadership of the Central Tibetan Administration. The move ended a 368-year tradition in which the Dalai Lamas held both spiritual and temporal power over Tibet. He described the devolution as the fulfillment of a long-held wish and a necessary step toward the modernization of Tibetan governance. He remains, in his own words and by his own preference, "a simple Buddhist monk."

Three Pillars · The Architecture of Teaching

Three commitments,
sustained for sixty years.

Across the decades of his public life, his message has remained remarkably stable. Not a doctrine, but three interlocking commitments — to be practiced, not merely believed.

01
Universal Compassion
བྱམས་པ་དང་སྙིང་རྗེ
The cultivation of genuine concern for the suffering of all sentient beings, not only humans. Compassion, in his teaching, is not sentiment but discipline — a daily practice that calms the mind, reduces anxiety, and ultimately benefits the practitioner as much as the recipient. The happier you are, the more you can help. The more you help, the happier you become. He has called this the "selfish altruism" — a recognition that helping others is the most reliable route to one's own happiness.
Lifetime practice
02
Non-violence
Ahimsa · མི་གནོད་པ་
The deliberate refusal to harm — in speech, action, or thought — even in the face of injustice. He has held this position through the loss of his country, the destruction of an estimated six thousand monasteries during the Cultural Revolution, the deaths of an estimated one million Tibetans in the decades following 1959. He has consistently refused to endorse armed resistance. Dialogue, never weapons. Reconciliation, never revenge. The Nobel Committee called this position "consistent resistance to the use of violence."
Since 1949
03
Universal Responsibility
ཀུན་གྱི་འགན་འཁྲི་
The recognition that the modern world — climate, weapons, pandemics, poverty, artificial intelligence — cannot be solved by any nation or ideology alone. Each person carries a share of the global burden. Good intentions are insufficient. Action is required. Small actions, multiplied across millions of individuals, become the future. He has spoken of this responsibility in addresses to the European Parliament, the United States Congress, the United Nations, and at countless universities.
Since 1973
The Lineage · 1391 — present

Six hundred years,
fourteen incarnations.

The institution of the Dalai Lama traces its origins to Gendun Drub, a 15th-century scholar, monk, and disciple of Tsongkhapa, founder of the Gelug school of Tibetan Buddhism. The title "Dalai Lama" — meaning "Ocean of Wisdom" in Mongolian — was conferred on the third incarnation by Altan Khan in 1578. The lineage has continued unbroken since 1391.

The Library · Selected Works

A body of writing,
1962 to the present.

His Holiness has authored or co-authored more than seventy books. They range from technical works on Buddhist philosophy, written in Tibetan, to bestselling co-authored volumes with Western psychologists, neuroscientists, and philosophers. The list below is a selection.

1962
My Land and My People
His first autobiographical account, written in exile, of Tibet before the Chinese occupation and his early years as Dalai Lama. Composed in Tibetan, translated and published in English.
EN · TIB
1966
The Opening of the Wisdom-Eye
A condensed presentation of the path to enlightenment, drawn from the Lamrim tradition of Tsongkhapa. A standard introductory text for serious students of Tibetan Buddhism.
EN
1988
The Dalai Lama at Harvard
Lectures given at Harvard University in 1981 on Buddhist paths to compassion, the role of altruism in human psychology, and the dialogue between Buddhist and Western thought.
EN
1990
Freedom in Exile
The second autobiography. Covers the years from the 1959 flight from Lhasa through the Nobel Peace Prize, including the formation of the government-in-exile in Dharamshala.
EN · 24 lang
1995
The Power of Compassion
A collection of teachings on the cultivation of bodhicitta — the awakened heart of compassion — and its role in daily life and ethical conduct.
EN
1998
The Art of Happiness
Co-authored with psychiatrist Howard Cutler. A handbook for living drawn from extended conversations. The book that introduced his thought to a broad Western lay audience; on bestseller lists for ninety-seven weeks.
EN · 50 lang
1999
Ethics for the New Millennium
An argument that ethics need not be grounded in religion, but in a shared human capacity for compassion. Written for a secular, global readership at the turn of the millennium.
EN · 30 lang
2005
The Universe in a Single Atom
On the convergence of Buddhist thought and modern science — a dialogue sustained across four decades through the Mind & Life Institute. Covers quantum physics, cosmology, consciousness, and ethics.
EN · 22 lang
2011
Beyond Religion
A call for a secular ethics grounded in shared human values — compassion, tolerance, restraint — independent of any particular faith tradition. A direct extension of his 1999 argument.
EN · 25 lang
2016
The Book of Joy
A week of conversations with Archbishop Desmond Tutu in Dharamshala, on lasting happiness in a suffering world. The two old friends laugh together often in these pages.
EN · 40 lang

A pocket-sized selection of teachings on suffering, impermanence, and the path of compassion. Drawn from public addresses given over the preceding decade.
EN · 18 lang

A historical reflection on the lineage of the Dalai Lamas across six centuries — written with scholar Alexander Norman. The institution viewed from the inside.
